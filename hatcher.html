Who Was Right About Scoring Runs — Hatcher or Dipoto?

When the Angels fired hitting coach Mickey Hatcher in 2012, GM Jerry Dipoto believed a more analytical, modern approach would produce better results. More walks, more patience, more theory. But what actually happened?

The Hatcher Era (2000–Mid 2012)

  • Average Runs per Season: ~778
  • Team Batting Average: Around .270
  • OPS often above .740 — peaking at .825 in 2000
  • Consistent offensive identity built around contact, aggression, and timely hitting

The Post-Hatcher Years (2013–2025)

  • Average Runs per Season: ~684
  • Team Batting Average dropped to .230–.245 range
  • Few seasons over 750 runs scored
  • Occasional OBP gains did not translate into more runs

Final Score: Hatcher 1, Dipoto 0

Despite the criticism of Hatcher’s “old-school” style, the numbers speak for themselves. The Angels consistently hit better and scored more with Hatcher in the dugout.

Modern analytics have their place, but when it comes to generating offense, Hatcher’s system produced results that Dipoto’s staff never replaced. A decade later, the team still hasn't recovered its offensive identity.
